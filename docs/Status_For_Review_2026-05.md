## What Scooby Is

A platform for writers to turn a story into a finished short vertical video — the kind of "microdrama" episode that plays on TikTok, Instagram Reels, and YouTube Shorts. Writer writes; Scooby produces. No film crew, no learning curve, no $500K budget.

Three quality tiers (writer experience is identical at all three):

| Tier | What it produces | Cost to us per 60s episode |
|------|------------------|------------------------|
| **Storyboard** (free) | Stylized AI illustrations + camera motion + narration | ~$0.25 |
| **Movie Lite** (paid) | Real animated video clips with character consistency | ~$3.50 |
| **Movie Pro** (premium) | Cinematic AI video with reference-image character anchoring | ~$13 |

---

## Where It Came From

Started with Joyce's stories and a real bottleneck: Joyce had material — observations from life, narratives with heart — but no production background and no realistic path to get them on screen. Existing options were either:

- **Too technical** — stitch together Gemini + Veo 3 + ChatGPT + ElevenLabs + CapCut by hand
- **Too generic** — general-purpose AI video tools that don't understand dramatic structure
- **Too expensive** — $500K traditional production budgets, gatekept by studios

The premise: a writer should be able to type a story and get a watchable video out the other end. Joyce became the design partner — every feature gets sanity-checked against "could a non-technical writer actually use this?"

## The Competitive Map

There are **two layers** to this market — distribution (where viewers watch) and production (how content gets made). Scooby plays in the production layer. Everyone in distribution is a potential customer or partner, not a competitor.

### Distribution platforms (where viewers watch)
ReelShort, DramaBox, Mansa, Hooked, Episode, PineDrama (TikTok), Vurt, Watch Club, Disney+ Verts, Netflix Clips. **All of them need content.** Their bottleneck is production speed — same as the producers feeding them. Vurt's pitch is even explicit about it: "48-72 hour upload-to-publish" because creators currently can't make content fast enough.

### Production-side (how content gets made) — where Scooby lives

| | Supercool Film Maker | Watch Club | Traditional studio (e.g. ReelShort original prod) | **Scooby** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Audience** | Aspiring directors | Union actors + writers | Pro production teams | Writers (any skill level) |
| **Production model** | AI-assisted DIY filmmaking | SAG-AFTRA/WGA talent producing live-action | Hire writers, shoot in 10-12 days, $50-150K/season | Writer types story → AI produces video |
| **Format scope** | Multi-format (features, TV, docs, vertical) | Live-action microdrama | Live-action microdrama | Vertical-drama specialist |
| **Members** | 130,000+ | New (April 2026) | n/a (B2B) | 2 (us) |
| **Pricing** | $8 entry + credits | TBD | n/a | TBD: free + Pro $19/mo + Premium $49/mo |
| **AI in pipeline** | Tools, not end-to-end | None (live actors) | Some, growing | End-to-end |

**Scooby's white space:** AI-end-to-end production specifically for the writer who doesn't want to be a director. Supercool's 130K members validate the broader category but their pitch is "you'll be a director" — that's a different person than Joyce. Watch Club is the union/live-action answer; we're the AI/no-budget answer.

## Where We're Going (Three Adjacent Markets)

The same writing → production engine opens three separate revenue paths. Joyce's stories can become any of these:

### 1. AI-Generated Vertical Dramas (the platform we're building)

Writer signs up, types a story, picks a style, gets a video to share, post, or submit to a distribution platform. Three quality tiers, monthly subscription.

**The market for this:** every distribution platform listed above (ReelShort, DramaBox, Mansa, Vurt, PineDrama, Watch Club, Hooked, Episode, Disney+ Verts, Netflix Clips) needs content. Scooby helps writers feed that pipeline at hundreds of times the speed of traditional production. Not all writers will go to those platforms — some just want to share to TikTok / IG Reels / YouTube Shorts directly. Both audiences work.

### 2. Scripts for Live Actors (a parallel revenue stream)

The same scene breakdown that drives our AI video can also be exported as a production-ready script for human actors. Vertical-drama production studios need a constant stream of new scripts; they hire writers but pay flat rates and own everything. Watch Club and traditional ReelShort productions both buy scripts — that's a real B2B opportunity.

Scooby's writers could:
- **License scripts directly to studios** — writer keeps a royalty, studio gets a production-ready script with built-in shot list
- **Sell scripts to indie filmmakers on Vurt** — Vurt explicitly courts indie creators. The 50/50 revenue split + 48-72 hour publishing window is friendly to spec-script producers
- **Use the AI version as a "spec demo"** — pitch human producers with a 60-second AI render before any cast or crew gets involved

The same software produces the script and the demo video. **The writer chooses the path.** Joyce might want the AI route for one story and a live-action sale for another.

### 3. B2B Tools-as-a-Service for Production Studios (further out)

Distribution platforms (Vurt, Mansa) and production studios are racing to ship more content faster. Scooby's pipeline could be packaged as B2B infrastructure at $500-2000/mo per studio. Different sales motion, much higher per-customer revenue, but later.

**The compound point:** one writer-facing platform feeds three distinct markets. We own the writer relationship; the writer's story can fan out into multiple commercial paths.

## Financial Projection (Conservative)

Three-year model. Numbers below are **deliberately on the cautious side** given the actual market size.

For perspective:
- Supercool reached 130,000+ members in roughly a year on a directors-first pitch
- ReelShort generates **~$1.2 billion / year** on the consumer side — a fraction of which routes back to production
- Vurt launched 2 months ago and already has 100+ original titles with marquee names

We're modeling Scooby at **far below** any of those trajectories.

### Pricing assumptions

| Tier | Monthly price | What's included |
|------|--------------|-----------------|
| **Free** | $0 | 2 Storyboard episodes/month |
| **Pro** | $19 | Unlimited Storyboard + 5 Movie Lite/month |
| **Premium** | $49 | Unlimited Movie Lite + 5 Movie Pro/month |
| **Studio** (B2B, year 2+) | $499+/mo | API access, white label, bulk credit pool |

### User and revenue projection

| End of | Free users | Paid users | Episodes / mo at peak | Monthly revenue at peak | Annual run-rate |
|--------|-----------|-----------|------------------------|--------------------------|-----------------|
| **Year 1** (2026) | 2,500 | 200 | ~1,200 | $5,400 / mo | **~$65K** |
| **Year 2** (2027) | 25,000 | 3,000 | ~20,000 | $85K / mo | **~$1.0M** |
| **Year 3** (2028) | 150,000 | 20,000 + 50 Studio customers | ~150,000 | $625K / mo | **~$7.5M** |

**Underlying assumptions:**
- Free → paid conversion: 7-10% (industry standard for creative SaaS is 5-15%)
- Pro vs Premium mix: 75% Pro / 25% Premium
- Churn: 8% monthly (typical early-stage SaaS)
- Year 3 includes 50 B2B Studio customers @ ~$1K/mo average = $50K/mo additional
- Even at Year 3, we're modeling Scooby at **~15% of Supercool's current member count** — leaves significant upside

### Cost-of-goods (per paid user, per month, blended average)

| Cost item | Year 1 estimate | Year 3 estimate (with scale) |
|-----------|----------------|------------------------------------------|
| AI image gen (TopView Nano Banana 2) | $1.50 | $0.40 (volume discount or annual commit) |
| AI voice (ElevenLabs) | $0.80 | $0.30 (direct contract at scale) |
| AI video (Seedance, Premium tier only) | $5.00 (when used) | $2.00 (Ultra annual or self-hosted Flux) |
| Infrastructure (Railway / Postgres / Redis) | $0.50 | $0.20 |
| **Total cost per paid user / month** | **~$3-7** | **~$1-2** |

**Implication:** at Year 3 scale (20,000 paid users at ~$24 avg revenue, ~$1.50 avg cost), gross margin per user is ~94%. Even Year 1 unit economics give ~70% gross margin.

### What we'd spend

- **Year 1 (2026):** $0 marketing budget (organic + Joyce's community), ~$80/mo infrastructure → ~$1K total. **Profit Year 1: ~$64K**
- **Year 2 (2027):** $100K/yr marketing (paid social, creator partnerships, conference presence), ~$2K/mo infrastructure → ~$124K total. **Profit Year 2: ~$880K**
- **Year 3 (2028):** $500K/yr marketing + 3 hires (~$300K total), ~$10K/mo infrastructure → ~$920K total. **Profit Year 3: ~$6.5M**

### Where we deliberately did NOT model upside

- **B2B script licensing revenue** (path #2) — could be $50-200/script × hundreds/year if studios bite, plus recurring royalties on produced scripts
- **Distribution partnerships** (Vurt, PineDrama, Mansa, Hooked) — they all want content. Could be revenue-share deals where they pay per episode delivered
- **Acquisition interest** — Adobe, Canva, Supercool, Lionsgate, or any of the streaming services may want vertical-drama specialization without building it themselves
- **Format goes mainstream** — already happening. If our infrastructure-for-writers position becomes valuable to Hollywood-tier producers, the projection floor is much higher

If any of those land, the numbers above significantly understate. If none land, the core SaaS still works and is profitable in Year 1.

## Why Now

Three things make this the moment, not 12 months from now or 12 months ago:

1. **The market just crossed the threshold** — $1.4B U.S. revenue in 2025 + every major streamer launching vertical-video products in 2026 + Issa Rae and Lifetime entering = mainstream credibility. We're entering with the wave, not selling against skepticism.
2. **AI production quality just got good enough** — six months ago Movie Lite quality wasn't there. Now it is, and Movie Pro (Seedance 2.0 with image references) is clean enough for actual viewer delivery. Production cost per episode is approximately the price of a fancy coffee.
3. **The competitive map has a hole exactly our shape** — distribution is crowded, production tools for directors exist (Supercool), live-action union production exists (Watch Club, traditional studios). **AI-end-to-end for writers — vertical-drama-native, no directing required — is wide open.**
