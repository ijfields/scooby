## What Scooby Is

A platform for writers to turn a story into a finished short vertical video — the kind that plays on TikTok, Instagram Reels, and YouTube Shorts. Writer writes; Scooby produces. No film crew, no learning curve, no $500K budget.

Three quality tiers:

| Tier | What it produces | Cost to us per 60s episode |
|------|------------------|------------------------|
| **Storyboard** (free) | Stylized AI illustrations + camera motion + narration | ~$0.25 |
| **Movie Lite** (paid) | Real animated video clips with character consistency | ~$3.50 |
| **Movie Pro** (premium) | Cinematic AI video with reference-image character anchoring | ~$13 |

## Where It Came From

Started with Joyce's stories and a real bottleneck: Joyce had material — observations from life, narratives with heart — but no production background and no realistic path to get them on screen. Existing options were either:
- **Too technical** (stitch together Gemini Business + Veo 3 + ChatGPT + ElevenLabs + CapCut by hand)
- **Too generic** (general-purpose AI video tools that don't understand dramatic structure)
- **Too expensive** ($500K traditional production budgets)

The premise: a writer should be able to type a story and get a watchable video out the other end. Joyce became the design partner — every feature gets sanity-checked against "could a non-technical writer actually use this?"

Supercool's 130K members validate the broader market. Scooby's narrower focus on vertical drama + writer-as-customer (not director-as-customer) is our defensible positioning. They're the closest competitor we've identified, but they're not chasing the same person.

## Where We're Going (Three Adjacent Markets)

The same writing → production engine opens three separate revenue paths. Joyce's stories can become any of these:

### 1. AI-Generated Vertical Dramas (the platform we're building)

Writer signs up, types or imports a story, picks a style, gets a video to share or post. Subscription pricing, three quality tiers. This is the core product and the first revenue stream.

**Market sized in millions today**, projected to grow into hundreds of millions before the end of the decade. ReelShort and DramaBox proved demand. The opportunity is to be the production-side platform — the one writers use *to make the content* — rather than competing with ReelShort/DramaBox on viewer attention.

### 2. Scripts for Live Actors (a parallel revenue stream)

The same scene breakdown that drives our AI video can also be exported as a production-ready script for human actors. Vertical-drama production studios (the ones following the ReelShort model) need a constant stream of new scripts; they hire writers but pay flat rates and own everything.

Scooby's writers could:
- **License scripts directly to studios** — writer keeps a royalty, studio gets a production-ready script with built-in shot list
- **Sell scripts to indie filmmakers** — smaller market but higher per-script price
- **Use the AI version as a "spec demo"** to pitch human producers — turn a 60-second AI render into a calling card before any humans get involved

The same software produces the script and the demo video. **The writer chooses the path.** Joyce might want the AI route for one story and the live-action sale for another.

### 3. B2B Tools-as-a-Service for Production Studios (further out)

Vertical-drama production houses currently use a patchwork of separate tools (script software, storyboard software, AI image gen, video gen, edit software). Scooby's pipeline could be packaged as B2B infrastructure for those studios at $500-2000/mo. Different sales motion, much higher per-customer revenue, but later.

**The compound point:** one writer-facing platform feeds three distinct markets. We own the writer relationship; the writer's story can fan out into multiple commercial paths.

## Conservative Financial Projection

Three-year model. Numbers below are **deliberately on the cautious side** — this is the slow-growth scenario, not the optimistic one. (Reference point: Supercool reached 130K+ members. We're modeling much slower growth than that.)

### Pricing assumptions

| Tier | Monthly price | What's included |
|------|--------------|-----------------|
| **Free** | $0 | 2 Storyboard episodes/month |
| **Pro** | $19 | Unlimited Storyboard + 5 Movie Lite/month |
| **Premium** | $49 | Unlimited Movie Lite + 5 Movie Pro/month |
| **Studio** (B2B, year 2+) | $499+/mo | API access, white label, bulk credit pool |

### User and revenue projection

| End of | Free users | Paid users | Episodes / mo at peak | Monthly revenue at peak | Annual run-rate |
|--------|-----------|-----------|------------------------|--------------------------|-----------------|
| **Year 1** (2026) | 1,500 | 100 | ~600 | $2,700 / mo | **~$32K** |
| **Year 2** (2027) | 15,000 | 1,500 | ~10,000 | $40K / mo | **~$485K** |
| **Year 3** (2028) | 75,000 | 10,000 + 25 Studio customers | ~75,000 | $300K / mo | **~$3.6M** |

**Underlying assumptions:**
- Free → paid conversion: 7-10% (industry standard for creative SaaS is 5-15%)
- Pro vs Premium mix: 75% Pro / 25% Premium
- Churn: 8% monthly (typical for early-stage SaaS; bakes in significant turnover)
- Year 3 includes 25 B2B Studio customers @ $1K average = $25K/mo additional
- Even at Year 3, we're modeling Scooby at **less than 8% the size of Supercool's current member count** — leaves significant room to be wrong on the upside

### Cost-of-goods (per paid user, per month, blended average)

| Cost item | Year 1 estimate | Year 3 estimate (with scale negotiation) |
|-----------|----------------|------------------------------------------|
| AI image gen (TopView Nano Banana 2) | $1.50 | $0.40 (volume discount or annual) |
| AI voice (ElevenLabs) | $0.80 | $0.30 (direct contract at scale) |
| AI video (Seedance, Premium tier only) | $5.00 (when used) | $2.00 (Ultra annual or self-hosted Flux) |
| Infrastructure (Railway / Postgres / Redis) | $0.50 | $0.20 |
| **Total cost per paid user/month** | **~$3-7** | **~$1-2** |

**Implication:** at Year 3 scale (10,000 paid users at ~$24 average revenue per paid user, ~$1.50 average cost per paid user), gross margin per user is ~94%. Even at Year 1 scale with much worse unit economics, gross margin is still ~70%.

### What we'd spend (also conservative)

- **Year 1 (2026):** $0 marketing budget — organic + Joyce's own community/social, $80 / mo infrastructure → run-rate ~$1K total. **Profit Year 1: ~$31K.**
- **Year 2 (2027):** $50K/yr marketing (paid social + creator partnerships), $500 / mo infrastructure → ~$56K total. **Profit Year 2: ~$430K.**
- **Year 3 (2028):** $300K/yr marketing + 2 hires ($200K total), $5K/mo infrastructure → ~$560K total. **Profit Year 3: ~$3M.**

**Takeaway:** even with deliberately conservative growth, the platform pays for itself in Year 1, becomes meaningfully profitable in Year 2, and crosses the threshold where outside funding becomes optional rather than necessary in Year 3.

### Where we deliberately did NOT model upside

- **B2B script licensing revenue** (path #2 above) — could be $50-200 per script × 100s of scripts/year if studios bite, plus recurring royalties on licensed scripts that get produced
- **Live-action production partnerships** (path #3) — much later, much bigger checks, not modeled at all
- **Vertical drama format moves mainstream** — if the format hits Hollywood-attention scale, our infrastructure-for-writers position is genuinely valuable to a much larger audience than projected
- **Acquisition interest** — multi-format AI platforms (Supercool, Adobe, Canva) may want to add vertical-drama specialization without building it themselves

If any of those land, the numbers above significantly understate the case. If none of them land, the core SaaS still works and is profitable in Year 1.
